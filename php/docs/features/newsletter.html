Newsletter

Collect subscribers with a drop-in signup form, confirm them with a double opt-in flow, and broadcast rich-text updates through Resend. Your audience is the union of confirmed standalone subscribers and Lodge members who opted in — compose once, send to everyone.

Overview

The Newsletter feature has three moving parts, all handled by a single PHP file (newsletter.php): a subscriber list with a double opt-in lifecycle, a signup form you drop into any theme, and a Resend-backed sender for test emails and full broadcasts. You bring a free Resend API key; Outpost handles confirmation emails, unsubscribe links, batching, and the RFC 8058 one-click unsubscribe headers that keep you out of spam folders.

Recipients are computed as a union of two groups: standalone subscribers who confirmed their email, and member-role users (Lodge members) who ticked the newsletter_optin flag on their account. A person who appears in both is de-duplicated by email — the standalone subscriber record wins, so their personal unsubscribe token is used.

One integration, one key. Everything runs through Resend. The API key is encrypted at rest (Ranger crypto) and used only server-side — it is never exposed to the browser or to the signup form. Without a key connected, the admin Newsletter page loads but sending is blocked with a clear prompt.

Connecting Resend

Go to Settings → Integrations → Newsletter (Resend) and fill in three fields:

Verify a sending domain

To send from your own address, add a domain in the Resend dashboard and publish the DNS records it gives you — typically an SPF record and a DKIM record (and a return-path/MX entry for bounce handling). Once Resend marks the domain verified, set your From Address to an address on that domain. This is what gets your mail delivered and lets recipients see your brand as the sender.

Sandbox fallback (no domain yet)

If you leave the From Address blank, Outpost falls back to Resend’s shared sandbox sender Outpost <onboarding@resend.dev>. This lets you send test emails to yourself before your domain is verified, so you can preview the layout end-to-end. The sandbox address is fine for testing but should not be used for real broadcasts — verify a domain first.

Verify before you broadcast. The sandbox sender is rate-limited and clearly marked as Resend’s test address. Real subscribers should receive mail from a verified domain of your own, or deliverability (and trust) will suffer.

Adding a signup form to a theme

The signup form is a self-contained marker element. Drop this anywhere in a theme template — a footer, a sidebar, a dedicated page — and Outpost’s engine injects the small client script (newsletter-client.js) automatically when it sees the marker on the page:

<div data-outpost-newsletter
     data-label="Subscribe"
     data-placeholder="you@example.com"></div>

The client builds a minimal, unstyled form (an email input plus a submit button and a live-region status line) inside the marker. Both attributes are optional:

AttributeDefaultPurpose
data-labelSubscribeText on the submit button.
data-placeholderyou@example.comPlaceholder for the email input.

On submit, the form POSTs the email to newsletter/subscribe with source: "form" and shows the returned message inline (for example, “Almost there — check your email to confirm.”). It renders no CSS of its own beyond the class hooks outpost-newsletter-form and outpost-newsletter-msg, so you style it to match your theme.

Tip — The form always reports success-shaped, neutral messaging and never reveals whether an address is already on the list. That’s deliberate: it prevents the form from being used to probe who has subscribed.

The double opt-in flow

No one is added to the list until they click a link in their inbox. The lifecycle has three public steps, all on the API and requiring no login.

  1. Subscribe. A visitor submits the form. Outpost creates (or resets) a pending subscriber with a random confirmation token and emails them a “Confirm your subscription” message containing the confirm link. Nothing is sent to this address by a broadcast while it is pending.
  2. Confirm. The visitor clicks the link, which hits newsletter/confirm?token=…. Outpost flips the record to confirmed, clears the token, and shows a simple “Subscription confirmed” page. They are now a recipient.
  3. Unsubscribe. Every email carries a per-recipient unsubscribe link (newsletter/unsubscribe?token=…). Visiting it marks the subscriber unsubscribed and shows a confirmation page. They receive nothing further.

Abuse protection on subscribe

The public subscribe endpoint is hardened so it can’t be used to spam confirmation emails at arbitrary inboxes:

  • Per-email cooldown — if an address is already pending and was created less than 10 minutes ago, Outpost returns the same “check your email” message without sending another confirmation. Re-subscribing after that window issues a fresh token.
  • Per-IP rate limit — a sliding window of 10 requests per hour per IP. Once exceeded, the endpoint still responds with the neutral pending message but sends no email.
  • Already confirmed — an address that’s already on the list gets a quiet “you’re already subscribed” response, not a new confirmation.
  • Email validation — addresses are validated and capped at 254 characters; invalid input returns a 400.

RFC 8058 one-click unsubscribe

Every broadcast email includes standards-based list headers so that Gmail, Apple Mail, and Outlook can show a native Unsubscribe button:

List-Unsubscribe: <https://yoursite/outpost/api.php?action=newsletter/unsubscribe&token=…>
List-Unsubscribe-Post: List-Unsubscribe=One-Click

When the mail client uses that button it sends a POST to the unsubscribe URL. Outpost’s unsubscribe handler accepts both a browser GET (which renders the confirmation page) and the one-click POST (which processes the token and returns an empty 200 with no page), satisfying RFC 8058. Confirmation tokens and unsubscribe tokens are validated as hex before any lookup.

Confirm and action links are absolute. They are built from your site_url setting. Make sure Settings has the correct public site URL, or the links in confirmation and broadcast emails will point at the wrong host.

Subscriber statuses

Every standalone subscriber row carries one of three statuses. Only confirmed subscribers receive broadcasts.

StatusMeaningReceives broadcasts?
pendingSigned up but hasn’t clicked the confirm link yet.No
confirmedClicked the confirmation link — a live recipient.Yes
unsubscribedOpted out via an unsubscribe link.No

Each subscriber also records its source (defaults to form), and timestamps for creation, confirmation, and unsubscription.

Member opt-ins

Lodge members are a separate pool. A member-role user with the newsletter_optin flag set on their account is treated as a recipient without going through the double opt-in flow — they already have a verified account relationship with the site. The recipient list merges these members with confirmed subscribers and de-duplicates by email. Internal/admin roles are always excluded, and members with an empty email are skipped.

Members don’t get a per-message token. An opted-in member’s unsubscribe link points at the generic unsubscribe endpoint rather than a personal token, so their unsubscribe is managed through their account/opt-in setting rather than a one-click list removal. Standalone subscribers always get a personal, tokenized unsubscribe link.

The admin Newsletter page

Open Newsletter in the admin. The page shows live stats, a composer, and the subscriber table.

Stats

Four counters across the top: Confirmed, Pending, Members opted in, and a highlighted Total recipients — the de-duplicated size of the send. The broadcast button is disabled when total recipients is zero.

Compose → test → broadcast

  1. Write. Enter a Subject and compose the body in the rich-text editor. The body HTML is wrapped in Outpost’s responsive email layout (header, content, and an unsubscribe footer) at send time — you write content, not a full HTML email.
  2. Send a test. Enter any address in the test field and click Send test. Outpost sends a single email to that address, subject-prefixed with [Test], using the same layout the real send will use. Test sends work even before a domain is verified (via the sandbox sender).
  3. Broadcast. Click Send to N subscribers. A confirmation step asks you to confirm the recipient count, then the broadcast goes out to the full subscriber + member union. On success the composer clears and a toast reports how many of how many recipients were sent.

Subscriber table

Below the composer, the most recent subscribers (up to 500) are listed with email, a colored status badge, source, and join date. If the list is empty, the page points you to add a data-outpost-newsletter form or opt members in.

Sending goes out in batches. Broadcasts are chunked at 100 recipients per request to Resend’s batch endpoint. Each chunk carries an Idempotency-Key (derived from the send id and chunk index) so a retried request won’t double-send that chunk. Each recipient still gets their own personalized unsubscribe header. If Resend returns a rate-limit (HTTP 429) mid-broadcast, the send is marked rate_limited and the count sent so far is recorded.

Free-tier limits

Resend’s free tier allows roughly 3,000 emails per month and 100 per day. Because every recipient counts as one email, a 100-recipient broadcast uses your entire daily allowance on the free tier. Plan larger lists around a paid Resend plan, or stagger sends across days. Confirmation and test emails also count against the quota.

Rate limits surface as errors. If a broadcast trips Resend’s limits, the admin sees “Resend rate limit hit — some emails may not have sent” (HTTP 429). Any recipients already sent in earlier chunks are not re-sent when you retry, thanks to the idempotency keys.

Endpoints

Three routes are public (no auth) and power the subscriber lifecycle; the rest are on the admin API and require capabilities. All are under api.php?action=….

RouteMethodAuthDescription
newsletter/subscribePOSTPublicSign up an email. Body { email, source? }. Creates a pending subscriber and sends the confirmation email. Per-email 10-min cooldown + per-IP rate limit (10/hour). Returns a neutral pending / already_subscribed status.
newsletter/confirmGETPublicConfirm a subscription. Query token. Flips pending → confirmed and renders an HTML confirmation page.
newsletter/unsubscribeGET / POSTPublicRemove a subscriber. Query/body token. GET renders a page; POST is the RFC 8058 one-click path and returns an empty 200.
newsletter/settingsGET / PUTsettings.*Read/write the Resend config. GET returns the decrypted key, from, and reply-to. PUT accepts resend_api_key (stored encrypted), newsletter_from, newsletter_reply_to.
newsletter/subscribersGETsettings.*List subscribers (latest 500) plus counts by status, member opt-in count, and total de-duplicated recipients.
newsletter/sendPOSTcontent.*Send. Body { subject, html, test?, test_email? }. With test: true sends one [Test] email to test_email; otherwise broadcasts to the full recipient union. Returns { sent, recipients }.
Public routes are token-gated, not login-gated. Confirm and unsubscribe are safe to expose because they act only on a valid, hex-validated random token tied to a single subscriber — there is no enumeration surface and no session required.

Data model

Three tables plus one column, created on demand by ensure_newsletter_tables():

  • subscribers — one row per standalone subscriber: email (unique), status, confirm_token, unsub_token, source, and lifecycle timestamps.
  • subscribe_rate_limits — per-IP timestamp windows backing the subscribe rate limiter.
  • newsletter_sends — a record of each broadcast: subject, html, recipient count, sent count, and status (sending, sent, or rate_limited).
  • users.newsletter_optin — an integer flag on the member/users table marking members who opted into the newsletter.
